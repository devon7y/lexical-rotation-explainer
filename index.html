Which way should the axes point?

Factor rotation, and what happens when the words get a vote.

The last unexamined choice in factor analysis. When researchers factor-analyze a questionnaire, they get a map: every item becomes a point, and items that people answer alike sit near each other. But the data fix only the points — not the axes drawn through them. Rotate the axes any way you like and the model fits your data exactly as well.

An item's “loadings” are just its coordinates on whichever axes you chose. The factor a researcher eventually names — “Depression”, “Extraversion” — is an axis, and where it points is pure convention. Rotation methods are the rules we use to choose.

Criterion meters — what each method scores

The formulas being computed live
With axis angles θ₁, θ₂, each axis is a unit vector u = [cos θ, sin θ] and T = [u₁ u₂]. Loadings are L = A₀ · (T⁻¹)ᵀ and factor correlations Φ = TᵀT. Then:
· varimax (maximize): Σ over factors of var(squared loadings in that column)
· quartimin (minimize): Σᵢ L²ᵢ₁·L²ᵢ₂ — the cross-loading product
· geomin (minimize): Σᵢ √((L²ᵢ₁+.01)(L²ᵢ₂+.01))
· leximax nameability: mean cosine between each axis's meaning direction and its retrieved term's embedding — needs the 369,703-word lexicon, so it's read from the precomputed grid. Meters are scaled to the range each criterion takes over all axis orientations.

Five rules for pointing the axes

Since every orientation fits equally well, each rotation method is just a criterion — a score that says which orientation to prefer. Watch each one pull the axes from a random start to its favorite answer.

Four rules, one blind spot. Varimax, quartimin, geomin and target rotation look only at the arithmetic pattern of the loading numbers. The words of the items never enter the computation — even though the entire purpose of rotation is to make the factors verbally interpretable. Leximax makes the words the criterion.

Case studies

The DASS measures depression, anxiety and stress with 42 items (a three-factor map). Here are its loadings under the conventional default (oblimin) and under leximax, with each factor column headed by the name the lexicon retrieves for it. Hover a row for the item text.

Reorienting the axes cannot change what the items say. The same three names appear under both orientations — and the factor the manual calls “Stress” reads as irritability either way. That disagreement is a fact about the scale's items, not an artifact of axis orientation. Rotation-invariant naming turns into a diagnostic: it tells you what your scale actually measures.

Two very different Big Five solutions: one from human responses (N = 874,434 people answering the items) and one response-free semantic solution (built from the items' text alone, no people at all). How well do their five factors line up? Each cell is the congruence |φ| between a human factor (rows) and a semantic factor (columns) — the diagonal is the matched pairs.

The lexicon acts as a shared coordinate system. Rotating both solutions with leximax — toward the same 369,703-word vocabulary — brings a human-response structure and a purely semantic one into near-perfect agreement.
