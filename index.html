Which way should the axes point?

Factor rotation, and what happens when the words get a vote.

The last unexamined choice in factor analysis. When researchers factor-analyze a questionnaire, they get a map: every item becomes a point, and items that people answer alike sit near each other. But the data fix only the points — not the axes drawn through them. Rotate the axes any way you like and the model fits your data exactly as well.

An item's “loadings” are just its coordinates on whichever axes you chose. The factor a researcher eventually names — “Depression”, “Extraversion” — is an axis, and where it points is pure convention. Rotation methods are the rules we use to choose.

Building blocks: how to read the map

Before any method, three simple ideas. Take them one at a time and the rest falls into place. (The map just below uses your real questionnaire; the sketches in steps 1–2 switch to a clean, made-up two-cluster example so the mechanics are easy to see.)

First: the map is a map of your questions

Here is the real PTQ map. The one thing to hold onto: each dot is one questionnaire item — one question — not one person. The hundreds of people who took the survey have been boiled down into where the questions land.

A question sits where it does because of how people answered it. Two questions land close together when people tend to answer them alike; a question drifts off on its own when its answers don't track the rest. A factor is nothing more than a direction that a bunch of dots line up along.

  • A dot's two coordinates are the item's “loadings” — its address on the map. Hover any dot to read them off the two axes.
  • Distance from the centre = how much the factors explain that item at all. Far out = well captured; hugging the centre = mostly noise.
  • The two axes drawn here are just the starting orientation the software happened to pick — which way they should point is the whole question this page is about.

1

A “loading” is how far an item reaches along an axis

Pick a direction and call it a factor. An item's loading on that factor is how far the item reaches along that axis — how strongly it lines up with that direction. Aim the axis straight at an item and its loading is large; swing the axis so it runs across the item, at right angles, and the loading shrinks to zero.

It is the part of the item that the axis captures — the reach along the axis, not the sideways gap left over to the side. Drag the axis handle below: the solid colored bar is the loading (the reach along the axis), and the loading chart tracks its length. The faint dashed line is only the construction — drop the item straight onto the axis to see where it lands. Point at the item → long reach; point away → almost none. The playground does this for two axes and all 28 items at once.

the one line of math
For an item at point p and an axis pointing in unit direction u = [cos θ, sin θ], the loading is the dot product p · u = pₓcos θ + p_y sin θ — the signed length of the item's reach along the axis (its projection). The leftover perpendicular distance is what this axis does not capture. With square, perpendicular axes this projection is exactly the loading; we let the axes tilt in step 3.

2

Rotation aims the axes. “Simple structure” aims them through the clusters

Now add the second axis. Since the points never move, choosing a rotation just means choosing where to aim the two axes through the fixed cloud.

Items that people answer alike sit together in clusters. Aim an axis straight through a cluster and every item in it gets a big loading on that axis and a near-zero loading on the other — each item is explained by one factor, not smeared across both. That clean pattern is what factor analysts call simple structure, and it is what every classical method is chasing.

Try the two presets. Messy aims the axes between the clusters, so almost every item loads middling on both (fat cross-loadings). Clean aims them along the clusters. Or drag the handle yourself.

Why it never gets perfectly clean — and the real trade-off. These two clusters aren't 90° apart, so perpendicular axes can't lie along both at once; the items between them get stranded, loading on both. Tilt the axes to follow the clusters and each item drops cleanly onto one factor — . The only catch: axes that lean toward each other describe factors that correlate.

Why we chase clean structure, and when to allow the factors to correlate

Why “simple structure”? It isn't just “big loadings.” It's each item loading big on one factor and near-zero on the rest. That pattern is what makes a factor interpretable: it's defined by a clean set of items, so you can read what it measures and give it a name. An item that loads on two factors at once belongs to neither and blurs both — you can't tell which factor it speaks for.

Orthogonal vs oblique is one question, not two. Perpendicular (orthogonal) rotation is just oblique rotation with the factor correlation handcuffed to zero. So letting the axes tilt can only match or beat the square solution on structure — you never trade cleanliness away for correlation. What you “pay” is only that you're now admitting the two factors are related.

So which do you prioritize? Uncorrelated factors are tidier to reason about and to score — each is an independent dimension, and their loadings read as plain item-factor correlations — which is why orthogonal was long the default. But real psychological constructs (anxiety and depression; the behavioural and emotional sides of procrastination here) usually do correlate, and forcing them apart distorts the picture. Modern practice: allow oblique by default. If the factors truly are independent it comes back at φ ≈ 0 and you've lost nothing; if they aren't, you've captured a real fact about the constructs. One caveat: if the tilt drives the correlation very high, that's a hint the two “factors” may really be one.

3

A rotation method is a score for “how simple?”, and a search for its best value

Here's the punchline. “Simple structure” is a feeling; a method turns it into a single number you can compute from the loadings — a criterion — and then aims the axes at the orientation that makes that number best.

The methods differ only in how they score simplicity. And because they score it differently, they disagree about where the axes should point. The next section makes that disagreement something you can see.

What each method optimizes — watch them disagree

Let's keep the axes square for a moment, so there is just one knob: the angle θ you turn the pair to. As you turn it, the loadings change — and so does every method's score. A factor is named from the items that load on it most — the top items shown above. Rotate the axes and different items rise to the top, so the retrieved name changes with them. That is why a different orientation gives a different name.

Where do these names actually come from?

Point the axis somewhere and a specific handful of items sit at its top (the “top items” above). Leximax turns those into a name automatically, in a frozen, deterministic pipeline:

  1. take the items that define the axis — its dominant pole, weighted by loading;
  2. average their meaning vectors (each item's text → a point in a language model's meaning-space);
  3. subtract the questionnaire's average meaning, so the name reflects what makes this factor distinctive within this survey;
  4. retrieve the nearest term from a 369,703-word construct lexicon.

The classical methods never do any of this — they fix the axis by the arithmetic of the loadings, and a researcher eyeballs the top items and names the factor by hand. Leximax closes the loop: it rotates the axis so that the name it would retrieve fits best. (The retrieval needs the full lexicon, so on this page the names are precomputed on a 3° grid of angles and looked up as you drag.)

Criterion meters — what each method scores

The formulas being computed live
With axis angles θ₁, θ₂, each axis is a unit vector u = [cos θ, sin θ] and T = [u₁ u₂]. Loadings are L = A₀ · (T⁻¹)ᵀ and factor correlations Φ = TᵀT. Then:
· varimax (maximize): Σ over factors of var(squared loadings in that column)
· quartimin (minimize): Σᵢ L²ᵢ₁·L²ᵢ₂ — the cross-loading product
· geomin (minimize): Σᵢ √((L²ᵢ₁+.01)(L²ᵢ₂+.01))
· leximax nameability: mean cosine between each axis's meaning direction and its retrieved term's embedding — needs the 369,703-word lexicon, so it's read from the precomputed grid. Meters are scaled to the range each criterion takes over all axis orientations.

Five rules for pointing the axes

Since every orientation fits equally well, each rotation method is just a criterion — a score that says which orientation to prefer. Watch each one pull the axes from a random start to its favorite answer.

Four rules, one blind spot. Varimax, quartimin, geomin and target rotation look only at the arithmetic pattern of the loading numbers. The words of the items never enter the computation — even though the entire purpose of rotation is to make the factors verbally interpretable. Leximax makes the words the criterion.

Case studies

The DASS measures depression, anxiety and stress with 42 items (a three-factor map). Here are its loadings under the conventional default (oblimin) and under leximax, with each factor column headed by the name the lexicon retrieves for it. Hover a row for the item text.

Reorienting the axes cannot change what the items say. The same three names appear under both orientations — and the factor the manual calls “Stress” reads as irritability either way. That disagreement is a fact about the scale's items, not an artifact of axis orientation. Rotation-invariant naming turns into a diagnostic: it tells you what your scale actually measures.

Two very different Big Five solutions: one from human responses (N = 874,434 people answering the items) and one response-free semantic solution (built from the items' text alone, no people at all).
